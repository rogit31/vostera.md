
Some tasks take significantly longer than others to run, for example when faced with the task of researching a powerful but rare foe, a party might want to find a local well stocked library with tomes depicting various monsters in their region. How does one make this part of an adventure engaging? You can just make your players make a single investigation or history roll sure, but that's not super engaging to roll over over again until they succeed or fail.

## Layers

Instead, one way of doing it is doing research in layers. Much like in real life, in general researching a specific topic starts with gathering sources. To do so, it starts with a cursory, wide search of different books that may or may not be related to the topic in question. For example, if your party wanted to research a very specific plant that only grows in cold caves of a specific region of the world, you might describe the cursory process of researching the topic as:

> You gather all books you think may or may not be related to the flower. Roll for investigation. Ok, so with a 12, you gather a bunch of books that you think may have something to say about this flower. You find a botanic collection of flowers of the area, one about the geology of caves, another about edible flowers, and a fourth about the cultural significance of plants in different cultures of the area. You're unsure which will be the most fruitful to read through first, what do you do?

This makes it so that everyday or at every roll of the research, the roll has a different impact. The first roll can be about the width of the search, the consequent rolls can be about eliminating unwanted sources, and then about the depth of the research, then about making connections for example. This makes it so that you can have different rolls have different outcomes on the entire process.

## Time limit

Research takes time. If there's a time crunch, that can make the impact of a single roll a lot more important. Make it so that rolls are a reflection of efficacy rather than success. A bad roll doesn't mean that you find nothing, it's that you pick the suboptimal way of researching the topic. You pick a book to read through that at first contains a promising introduction about the topic but ends up hemming and hawing about a different topic entirely. Or you realize as you're reading the book that the researcher in question is entirely wrong about the topic and you waste a day.
